Example 3. Clinical Trial

A pharmaceutical company wants to model the risk and expected cost of investing in a new drug candidate. The candidate has shown promising results in preclinical studies, but this does not guarantee that it will be safe, effective or approved in humans.

In real drug development, clinical trials are usually divided into sequential phases. Phase 1 mainly evaluates safety and tolerability, Phase 2 studies preliminary efficacy and dose selection, and Phase 3 confirms safety and efficacy in a larger patient population. If any phase fails, the development programme is halted and the following phases are not funded.

For this example, the company models the process as follows:

  • Phase 1 (Safety and tolerability): 60 participants receive the candidate drug. The main objective is to evaluate safety, tolerability, dose range and possible adverse effects. This phase has an estimated cost of 5 million euros. The probability of successfully advancing to Phase 2 is 52.0%.
  • Phase 2 (Proof of concept and dose selection): 200 patients with the target condition receive the drug or a comparator. The objective is to obtain preliminary evidence of efficacy, continue evaluating safety and select the most appropriate dose for larger studies. This phase has an estimated cost of 15 million euros. The probability of successfully advancing to Phase 3 is 28.9%.
  • Phase 3 (Pivotal efficacy and safety): 1,500 patients are enrolled to confirm the drug's efficacy, safety profile and benefit-risk balance at scale. This phase has an estimated cost of 45 million euros. The probability of successfully reaching regulatory filing is 57.8%.
  • Regulatory review: If the clinical dossier is submitted, the regulatory authority reviews the evidence on quality, safety and efficacy. The review process has an estimated cost of 5 million euros. The probability of approval after submission is 90.6%.

Therefore, the probability of successfully reaching regulatory approval can be calculated as:

    Probability of regulatory approval = 0.520 x 0.289 x 0.578 x 0.906 = 0.0787

Under these assumptions, the model estimates a 7.87% probability of regulatory approval from the beginning of Phase 1.

The expected total cost including regulatory review is:

    Expected clinical cost = 5 + (0.520 x 15) + (0.520 x 0.289 x 45) = 19.56 million euros

The expected total cost including regulatory review is:

    Expected total cost = 19.56 + (0.0787 x 5) = 20 million euros

From a portfolio perspective, the risk-adjusted expected cost per approved drug is:

    Risk-adjusted expected cost per approved drug = 20.00 / 0.0787 = 254.1 million euros

This does not mean that a single approved drug will always cost 254.1 million euros. It means that, if many similar candidates were developed under the same assumptions, the average cost per successful approval would be approximately 254.1 million euros.

Some questions that the model can answer are:

  • What is the probability that the drug will reach regulatory approval?
  • What is the expected clinical cost before regulatory filing?
  • What is the expected total cost including regulatory review?
  • What is the risk-adjusted expected cost per approved drug?
  • Which phase contributes most to the overall probability of failure?

As in the cybersecurity example, these values should not be interpreted as universal predictions. They are parameters of the model. In a real pharmaceutical company, they should be adjusted using indication-specific clinical data, historical trial outcomes, trial design, recruitment difficulty, regulatory pathway, safety profile and internal cost estimates.

Reference note: The phase-transition probabilities are based on BIO, Informa Pharma Intelligence and QLS Advisors' Clinical Development Success Rates 2011-2020 report. The description of the clinical phases follows the FDA's public description of the drug-review process. The cost values are simplified scenario parameters chosen within published phase-level clinical-trial cost ranges and do not include discovery, manufacturing scale-up, opportunity cost, post-market studies or the full cost of failed research programmes.
