RISK DSL

In an increasingly complex world, risks don't appear in isolation — they are interconnected, generating situations that are difficult to anticipate using traditional methods.

This project introduces an interactive web application that combines probabilistic models with visual diagrams, allowing users to represent uncertainty scenarios in a clear and intuitive way. Through a visual DSL built with Angular and TypeScript, anyone can transform abstract statistical calculations into understandable diagrams, with no advanced technical knowledge required.

The tool is designed for critical sectors such as space missions, banking and insurance, where making informed decisions can make a significant difference.

The goal is simple: to bring risk modelling closer to analysts and management teams, merging the rigour of data with the clarity of visuals, so that any organisation can understand its risks and act with greater confidence and security.

Binomial Examples

Example 1. In-line quality control

A factory produces screws on an automated production line. The quality control process consists of two stages: first, an automatic visual inspection (using a camera), and then, only if the camera detects a defect, a manual inspection by an operator. Historical data shows that:

  • 5% of the screws are defective.
  • The camera correctly identifies 90% of the defective screws (true positive rate).
  • If a screw is referred for manual inspection, the operator confirms the defect 95% of the time.

The aim of the model is to estimate the probability that a screw will be definitively rejected after passing through the entire process.

Example 2. Logistics Supply Chain

A logistics company has a supply chain consisting of three stages: supplier, warehouse and distribution. If any link fails, the order does not reach the customer. The probabilities of failure for each stage are:

  • Supplier: 3%
  • Warehouse: 5%
  • Distribution: 2%

What is the probability that an order will reach the customer successfully? What is the probability that an order will fail due to a supplier issue, given that it failed?

Example 3. Cybersecurity

A security team wants to model the risk of an external attacker compromising their system. The attack must overcome three barriers succesfully:

  • Firewall: 10% chance of being breached.
  • Intrusion Detection System (IDS): if the Firewall is breached, there is a 20% chance of being bypassed.
  • Two-factor Authentication: if the IDS is bypassed, there is a 5% chance of being compromised.

Although this probability seems low, in a system receiving 10.000 attack attempts per month: Expected succesful attacks = 10.000 * 0.1 * 0.2 * 0.05 = 10 successful attacks per month.

Example 3.5. Cybersecurity Extension

Furthermore, an extension of the previous example has been proposed where, upon realising that the system has been compromised, two key points are taken into account:

  • Detection: There is a 70% chance that the breach will be detected within the first 24 hours.
  • Mitigation: If the breach is detected, there is an 80% chance that effective mitigation measures will be implemented within the first 48 hours.

If both key points apply, even if there has been a data breach, it will have been contained early and the consequences will be minimal.

Example 4. Clinical Trial

A pharmaceutical company is conducting a clinical trial to evaluate the efficacy of a new drug. The trial consists of two phases:

  • Phase 1 (Safety): 100 patients receive the drug, and 70 show improvement.
  • Phase 2 (Preliminary Efficacy): 200 patients receive the drug, and 90 show improvement.
  • Phase 3 (Large-scale Efficacy): 500 patients receive the drug, and 300 show improvement.

Furthermore, after passing Phase 3, the regulatory authorities approves the drug with a 99% confidence level, which means that there is a 1% chance that the drug is not effective despite the positive results in the trial. The company wants to estimate the overall probability that the drug is effective, taking into account the results of all three phases and the regulatory approval process.

Bayes Examples

Example 1. Falcon 9 Mission

This example demonstrates how a Bayesian network can help estimate the risk of an aerospace mission based on partial evidence. The case is inspired by the Falcon 9, whose first stage is equipped with nine Merlin engines and is designed with engine-out capability: it can continue the mission even if one or two engines fail during ascent. The question the model answers is:

If an engine failure is detected during flight, what is the probability that the mission will succesfully reach orbit?

To avoid an overly large network, the model does not represent the 9 engines separately. If each engine were modelled individually, 2^9 = 512 possible combinations would have to be considered, which would make the probability tables difficult to manage in an interactive example.

To avoid having to manually enter the 9 engines into the network, the probability of 0, 1, 2 or more engines failing is calculated in advance using a binomial distribution.

In this case, two parameters are used:

  • Number of engines: 9
  • Probability of failure per engine: 0.7 %

Using these values, the model calculates the probability that no engines will fail, the probability that 1 or 2 engines will faily, and the probability that 3 or more engines will fail.

The model consists of five main nodes:

  • Partial Engine Failure: 1 or 2 engines fail. The mission can still succeed.
  • Critical Engine Failure: 3 or more engines fail. The mission is likely to fail.
  • Meteorological Conditions: Adverse weather can affect the mission's success.
  • Mission reaches orbit: The mission successfully reaches its intended orbit.
  • Payload release: The payload is successfully released once the mission reaches its target orbit.

Simplifying the engines allows the probabilistic logic of the real system to be retained without overcomplicating the model. Rather than manually entering hundreds of combinations, the binomial calculation summarises the probability of a partial or critical failure, and the Bayesian network is responsible for propagating that risk through the rest of the model to estimate the overall mission risk based on the evidence of an engine failure.

The model's structure reflects a simple decision chain: engine failures and weather conditions influence the probability of reaching orbit, and, in turn, reaching orbit determines the probability of succesfully deploying the payload.

This case illustrates the value of the Bayesian approach within the DSL: the user can see how the risk changes when new evidence is introduced. A partial failure does not automatically imply mission failure, but it does reduce the margins of success. Conversely, the combination of several adverse factors can significantly degrade the probability of success.

Probabilities defined in the CPTs

Each node in the network has an associated probability table. In root nodes, the table defines their initial probability. In dependent nodes, the CPT indicates how the probability changes according to the state of their parent nodes.

Example 2: Abort Launch at T-10

In the final second before launch, the rocket's software automatically checks several critical systems. If any of the sensors detects an anomaly, the system can trigger an automatic abort at T-0, halting the launch before ignition is complete.

The problem is that an alarm does not always indicate an actual physical fault. Sensors can generate false positives due to electrical noise, anomalous readings, temporary deviations or software errors. Therefore, when an alert is triggered, the flight controller needs to answer a key question:

If the abort system has been activated, what is the probability that there is actually a physical fault?

This example shows how a Bayesian network allows a binary alert — “abort” or “no abort” — to be transformed into a probabilistic estimate useful for decision-making

Context of the case

The model depicts a scenario inspired by real-world launches, where an anomalous reading at T-0 can temporarily halt the mission. In such scenarios, the objective is not merely to detect the alarm, but to assess whether that alarm corresponds to:

  • A genuine physical fault in the vehicle.
  • A false positive generated by the sensor system.
  • A combination of several weak signals.
  • Strong evidence, enough to warrant a full inspection.

In a risk analysis application, this example demonstrates the value of the DSL developed in the final year project: the user can visually construct the network, define the conditional probability tables and observe how the probability of a real failure changes when new evidence is introduced.

The network contains five main nodes:

  • Real System Failure: There is a real physical failure in one of the critical systems at T-0.
  • Propellant Sensor: The propellant sensor system reports an anomaly.
  • EC Sensor: The engine control system sensor reports an anomaly.
  • T-0 Alert: The software activates automatic abort.
  • Failure is real: The alert actually corresponds to a physical failure and not a false positive.

Probabilities defined in the CPTs

Node 1 · RealSystemFailure

This node represents the prior probability that an actual physical fault exists at T-0.

State Probability Meaning
yes 0.8% Initial probability of a real fault in one of the critical systems.
no 99.2% Complementary: no real physical fault exists.

Node 2 · PropellantSensor

This node models the response of the propellant sensor depending on whether a real fault exists.

RealSystemFailure P(PropellantSensor = yes) Interpretation
yes 90% If there is a real fault, the sensor detects it in 90% of cases.
no 4% If there is no real fault, the sensor can produce a false positive in 4% of cases.

This table separates two important concepts:

  • Sensitivity: ability to detect a fault when one exists.
  • False positive: probability of triggering an alarm even when there is no real fault.

Node 3 · ECSensor

The engine control system sensor also depends on the existence of a real fault.

RealSystemFailure P(ECSensor = yes) Interpretation
yes 95% If there is a real fault, the ECS sensor detects it with high probability.
no 3% If there is no real fault, a false alarm can occur in 3% of cases.

The ECS sensor is modelled as more sensitive and with a lower false positive rate than the propellant sensor.

Node 4 · T0Alert

This node represents the automatic decision made by the software. The alert is triggered with high probability if at least one of the sensors reports an anomaly.

PropellantSensor ECSensor P(T0Alert = yes) Interpretation
yes yes 99% Both sensors alarm; the abort is triggered almost with certainty.
yes no 95% Only the propellant system alarms.
no yes 93% Only the ECS system alarms.
no no 1% An abort may occur for other reasons: electrical noise, a bug or an external signal.

This CPT represents logic similar to an OR gate, but with uncertainty. No deterministic rule is enforced; instead, it allows the imperfections of the software and the environment to be modelled.

Node 5 · FailureIsReal

This node summarises whether the observed alert actually corresponds to a physical fault.

T0Alert P(FailureIsReal = yes) Interpretation
yes 15% With an active alert, there is a 15% probability that the fault is real.
no 0.3% Without an alert, it is very unlikely that an undetected physical fault exists.

This node is especially useful because it summarises the practical value of the model: the network does not only detect an alarm, it estimates whether that alarm should be considered a real fault or a false positive.

Example 3. Crew Dragon

This example models a critical phase of a crewed mission: the re-entry and recovery of the Crew Dragon capsule. Unlike a launch, where the main objective is to reach orbit, here the risk lies in the capsule successfully performing the de-orbiting manoeuvre, passing through the atmosphere, deploying its parachutes and landing safely.

The question the model answers is

Given the re-entry sequence, what is the probability that the crew will be recovered safe and sound?

The Crew Dragon re-entry can be divided into three main phases:

  • De-orbit burn: The capsule fires its thrusters to slow down and begin its descent.
  • Atmospheric re-entry: The capsule passes through the atmosphere, facing extreme heat and forces.
  • Descent and splashdown: The parachute system slows the capsule down before it hits the ocean, where it must be recovered by rescue teams.

In addition to the technical systems, ocean conditions also play a role. Even if the capsule functions correctly, rough seas can make splashdown difficult, cause it to capsize or delay recovery.

The model includes the following nodes:

  • Deorbit Burn Failure: The deorbit manoeuvre is not executed correctly.
  • Heat Shield Failure: The heat shield does not provide adequate protection during re-entry.
  • Parachute Deployment Failure: The parachute system does not deploy correctly.
  • Oceanic Conditions: Sea conditions are unsuitable for splashdown
  • Normal Splashdown: The capsule lands safely in the ocean.
  • Crew Rescued: The crew is successfully recovered.

Probabilities defined in the CPTs

Node 1 · DeorbitBurnFailure

This node represents the initial probability that the deorbit manoeuvre fails.

State Probability Interpretation
yes 0.5% The deorbit burn manoeuvre fails.
no 99.5% The manoeuvre is executed correctly.

Node 2 · HeatShieldFailure

The heat shield failure depends on whether the deorbit manoeuvre was correct.

DeorbitBurnFailure P(HeatShieldFailure = yes) Interpretation
yes 80% An incorrect trajectory can expose the shield to off-design conditions.
no 0.1% On a nominal trajectory, heat shield failure is highly unlikely.

This table represents a strong causal relationship: a failure in the re-entry trajectory drastically increases thermal risk.

Node 3 · ParachuteFailure

The parachute system depends on the prior state of the capsule after re-entry.

HeatShieldFailure P(ParachuteFailure = yes) Interpretation
yes 85% If the shield fails, the capsule can become unstable and hinder deployment.
no 0.2% With a nominal re-entry, parachute system failure is very unlikely.

This CPT lets us model how a thermal problem can turn into a descent problem.

Node 4 · OceanicConditions

This node represents an external factor: the sea state in the recovery zone.

State Probability Interpretation
yes 5% Oceanic conditions are adverse.
no 95% Conditions are favourable for recovery.

Unlike the technical nodes, this node does not depend on the capsule. It represents an environmental source of risk.

Node 5 · NormalSplashdown

Splashdown depends on the state of the parachute system and the oceanic conditions.

ParachuteFailure OceanicConditions P(NormalSplashdown = yes) Interpretation
no no 99.8% Everything works correctly and the sea is favourable.
no yes 75% The parachutes work, but the adverse sea reduces safety.
yes no 3% The parachutes fail, even though the ocean is in good condition.
yes yes 0.5% The parachutes fail and the sea is also adverse.

This table is key because it combines technical risk and environmental risk in a single operational outcome.

Node 6 · CrewRescued

The final state depends on whether splashdown occurred within safe parameters.

NormalSplashdown P(CrewRescued = yes) Interpretation
yes 99.8% If splashdown is nominal, safe recovery is almost certain.
no 20% Even with a non-nominal splashdown, there can be a survival margin.

This node summarises the final outcome of interest to the controller: the probability of safe crew recovery.
